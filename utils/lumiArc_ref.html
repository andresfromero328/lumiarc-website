What it is
LumiArc
An AI-powered college advisor for every student — providing personalized, campus-aware guidance across academic planning, financial aid, and career preparation. LumiArc knows the student's school, country, financial situation, and goals from day one, and proactively surfaces what they need to do next before they even know to ask.
LumiArc's core value proposition: counselors are hard to reach, have appointment wait times of weeks, and change mid-semester with no institutional memory. LumiArc is the constant — always available, always current, always aware of the student's full situation. It fills the gap between counselor appointments and makes those appointments more productive when they happen.

Document parsing — hybrid model
Why hybrid
Degree requirements PDFs are inconsistently formatted — dense paragraphs, footnotes, "choose 2 of 5" rules, co-requisites buried in fine print. No model gets this perfectly right every time. A silent parse that the student never reviews is a trust risk. LumiArc parses, presents, and asks the student to confirm before anything is locked in.
Parsing flow
Step 1 — Upload Student uploads transcript and degree requirements PDF
Step 2 — Parse GPT-4.1 reads both documents and generates structured Requirement objects. GPT-4.1 is used here — not a lighter model — because the OR logic, nested rules, and inconsistent formatting require stronger reasoning. This is a one-time cost per student.
Step 3 — Flag LumiArc flags anything it couldn't confidently extract: missing sections, OR choices that need student input, co-requisites, wrong document detected.
Step 4 — Review Student sees the extracted requirements in plain language, reviews each section, corrects anything wrong, and confirms. This is also where OR choices are resolved — "I took MATH 15A, not CSE 20."
Step 5 — Lock Confirmed data is locked. Powers the Checklist, Degree Path, and Semester Schedule from this point forward. Never touched by the parser again unless the student re-uploads.
Common flags LumiArc raises during review: missing university-wide requirements not in the PDF, OR choices that need student confirmation, co-requisites that must be taken together, and wrong document uploaded for the declared degree.

Accuracy & counselor principle
LumiArc is only as accurate as what goes in
Communicated clearly at onboarding and reinforced during the document review step. LumiArc doesn't hide uncertainty — if something looks wrong or incomplete, it says so. The student is always in control of their data.
LumiArc prepares students for counselors, not replaces them
For complex situations — transfers, major changes, petitions — LumiArc surfaces specific questions the student should bring to their counselor. A student who uses LumiArc walks into that appointment knowing exactly what to ask.
The counselor nudge is contextual, not a constant disclaimer. LumiArc only suggests "talk to your counselor" when the situation genuinely warrants it — not on every screen as a legal hedge.
Onboarding inputs
Residency / citizenship status Citizen, permanent resident, DACA, undocumented, F-1/J-1 visa — drives which financial aid features are shown or blocked entirely. FAFSA flow for citizens and permanent residents. California Dream Act Application flow for DACA / AB 540 eligible students. Institutional and private scholarship focus for undocumented students in states without Dream Act provisions. F-1/J-1 students: federal aid features blocked, state grant features blocked, work-study blocked — scholarship uploads, tracking, and targeted international scholarship references fully available. Permanent residents are functionally identical to domestic students for financial aid purposes.
FAFSA filed status and aid year Whether FAFSA has been filed and for which aid year. Drives deadline alerts and grant eligibility check.
Aid award letter upload Primary onboarding path for financial data. Sonnet parses the letter, extracts grants, loans, work-study allocation, and disbursement schedule. Student reviews and confirms before anything is locked. Free once per aid year — mid-year re-uploads are paid. Same hybrid parse-and-confirm flow as degree requirements.
Scholarship PDF upload Student uploads any scholarship documents they have — brochures, award letters, their own lists. Sonnet parses, extracts structured records, flags missing or ambiguous fields, student confirms. Free with a passive rate limit of 2–3 uploads per month. Virtually no legitimate student hits this limit given only 7–11% of students receive scholarships and most have one or two.
LumiArc is only as accurate as what goes in. Both uploads go through the hybrid confirm flow — LumiArc parses and presents, student reviews and corrects, data is locked only after confirmation. Communicated clearly at onboarding.
DACA and undocumented students face a fundamentally different financial aid landscape — no federal aid eligibility, state programs vary widely, institutional aid depends entirely on the school. The model knows the general landscape but accuracy risk is higher here than anywhere else in this pillar. Always search for current state program rules before answering, and the counselor nudge is more warranted for these students than anywhere else. A dedicated undocumented student resource center at their school is the most valuable resource LumiArc can point them to.
F-1/J-1 students: all federal and state aid features are blocked at the routing level — LumiArc does not attempt to reason about eligibility it knows doesn't apply. What LumiArc does instead: confirms their status clearly at onboarding, explains what isn't available and why, and routes them to the right resources — their school's international student financial aid office, plus scholarship references filtered for international students (IEFA, institutional international aid pages, private databases without citizenship restrictions). Document uploads and scholarship tracking work identically to domestic students. Award letters for international institutional aid may not include federal fields — the parser extracts what's present and the confirm flow handles gaps.

Pillar philosophy
Protect first, optimize second
The primary job is making sure students don't lose money they're entitled to — missed deadlines, eligibility lapses, SAP violations. Scholarship matching and optimization are valuable but secondary to not letting the floor fall out.
Translate, don't just inform
Every piece of financial aid information should be rendered in plain language with context. Not "your SAI is 1,500" but what that number means, why it matters, and what the student should do about it.
Know the limits and say so
Financial aid has edge cases everywhere — appeals, professional judgment, unusual dependency situations, institutional aid with its own rules. LumiArc is confident where rules are clear and explicit about where the student needs their school's financial aid office.
This pillar is structurally leaner than academic planning — fewer persistent artifacts, more AI counselor and proactive alerts. That's intentional. Most financial aid interaction is periodic, not daily. The value here is clarity and protection, not constant engagement.
Where financial aid knowledge comes from
What the model already knows
AI models know federal financial aid well — FAFSA structure, Pell Grant eligibility rules, subsidized vs unsubsidized loans, SAP requirements. This is well-documented public information. For explaining how the system works and general eligibility questions, the model is reliable.
Where accuracy risk is real
Three areas require live data — never rely on training data alone. Federal rules that change year to year (exact Pell maximums, SAI thresholds, loan limits). State programs (Cal Grant deadlines, verification requirements change every cycle). Institutional aid (each school has its own scholarships, renewal criteria, appeals process — unreliable in training data for all but large schools).
Deadline data is public — every US institution publishes it on their financial aid and registrar pages. The problem is not availability, it's maintenance at scale. LumiArc solves this with on-demand web search rather than a maintained database. See Decision 4.

Backend infrastructure
Supabase handles everything
All financial aid backend logic runs on Supabase — PostgreSQL database for student aid profiles and scholarship records, Edge Functions for scheduled background searches and alert logic, and push notifications triggered directly from Edge Functions via Expo's push notification API. No separate server or Firebase needed. One service, one bill.
The main cost variable at scale is AI model API calls, not infrastructure. Supabase free tier covers early development. Pro tier is $25/month flat when LumiArc outgrows it.
Onboarding inputs
The career pillar runs almost entirely off data already collected at onboarding — target role, graduation timeline, year, major, and institution. One additional input is specific to this pillar.
Resume upload Optional at onboarding — the pillar works without it, but resume feedback and career roadmap gap analysis both require it. PDF or Word doc. Upload is free with no replacement limit — resumes change constantly and re-parsing on replacement is cheap (one GPT-4.1 call per upload, same as a syllabus). Parsed at feedback time, not at upload. The free/paid line is drawn at reasoning depth, not upload access.
No upload limits on either plan. The cost argument for limiting uploads doesn't hold — parsing is ~$0.02–0.05 per resume. Artificially limiting re-uploads creates friction on the one action you most want students to take (keeping their resume current in LumiArc). The wall is at reasoning depth, not at the upload gate.

Group 2 — Resume feedback
Two-pass model
Resume feedback is the highest-priority feature in this pillar and the clearest demonstration of LumiArc's value over generic resume tools. The competitive moat is context — LumiArc knows the student's major, target role, year, and internship timeline. A score anyone can get elsewhere for free; contextualized feedback tied to the student's specific situation is what LumiArc uniquely provides.
Pass 1 — Structured review
Triggered at upload. GPT-4.1 parses the resume and generates a structured score (0–100) and section-level grades across: impact (quantified achievements), experience (relevance to target role), skills (keyword coverage for the target role), and formatting (ATS compatibility, readability). Output is stored — not re-run unless the resume is replaced. Cost is one GPT-4.1 call per upload (~$0.02–0.05).
Free — score + section grades GPT-4.1 — parse only (one-time)
Pass 2 — Contextual reasoning
On-demand when the student asks a follow-up. LumiArc reasons against both the parsed resume structure and the student's full profile. Examples: "What's the weakest section?", "How would this read to a Google recruiter?", "What's missing for a PM role?" LumiArc can rewrite weak bullets on request, tailor feedback to target company tier, and answer follow-up questions in conversation. A CS sophomore targeting a summer 2026 SWE internship gets different feedback than a senior targeting a PM role at a startup. This is the feature that earns its place — a first-gen student who has never had their resume reviewed by anyone in the industry gets feedback a counselor appointment might not deliver.
Paid GPT-4.1 → GPT-5 Advanced
The free/paid split on resume feedback is the acquisition hook for this pillar. The student uploads their resume, gets a score, sees a section is weak, and hits the wall when they try to understand why. Same conversion logic as the Degree Path and Career Roadmap example previews — they see that something needs fixing but the actionable detail is one payment away.
Group 3 — Career roadmap (Paid)
What it is
The Complex-tier feature for this pillar. Structurally analogous to Degree Path — a multi-semester view, always current, handles what-if scenarios. The primary upgrade moment for Career Prep. LumiArc reasons over three inputs: the student's current state (year, major, completed coursework, internship history, resume), their target role (job title, company tier, timeline), and what that role actually requires (skills, experience signals, credential expectations). Output has two parts: a gap analysis and a semester-by-semester action plan.
Paid GPT-5 Complex
Gap analysis
A clear, specific comparison of where the student is versus what their target role requires. Not generic ("you need leadership experience") — tied to their actual profile ("you have no industry experience yet, your CS coursework is on track but you're missing a data structures course that often appears in SWE interview loops, and your resume has no projects listed"). Structured breakdown by category: technical skills, experience signals, credentials, timeline.
Semester-by-semester action plan
Starting from the student's current semester, LumiArc maps what needs to happen each semester to close the gap by the target date. Stack strategy
All stagesUse the optimized multi-provider stack from day one. Prompts are tuned for the models they'll actually run on in production — no re-validation needed later. Costs are negligible at early scale ($5–30/mo).
Prompt cachingEnabled from day one. Layers 1 + 4 (~1,200 tokens of static prompt) are identical across every call for every user — perfect cache candidates. Saves 30–35% on input token costs. The dollar impact grows with scale but costs nothing to enable early.
The stack is provider-agnostic. LumiArc's prompt layers (persona, student context, live data, behaviour rules) work across any model. No vendor lock-in. Swap models as pricing changes or better options emerge — the routing tier structure stays the same.

Downgraded stack — prompt and context control
Two levers control depth
When a free user exceeds their rolling token budget, all queries route to DeepSeek V3.2. The quality difference is controlled through prompt instructions and context injection — not model capability. Two levers work together to ensure the downgraded experience is safe, accurate, and clearly limited.
Layer 4 — behaviour rules swap
On the full stack, Layer 4 instructs: "When a query has implications across academic, financial, or career pillars, identify and address all affected areas. Connect the dots for the student." On the downgraded stack, Layer 4 swaps to: "Answer the student's question within the current pillar. If the question may have implications in other pillars, name which pillars may be affected but do not analyze the specifics. Direct the student to the full AI for detailed analysis."
Layer 2 — context trimming
On the full stack, Layer 2 includes the student's complete profile — academic progress, financial aid, career goals. On the downgraded stack, Layer 2 can be trimmed to only inject context relevant to the current screen. If the student is on the academic tab, only academic data is injected. The model cannot hallucinate cross-pillar connections to data it doesn't have in the prompt.
Middle-ground cross-pillar flagging
The downgraded stack does not attempt detailed cross-pillar analysis — but it does flag risks. This is the middle ground between full analysis and silence. On the full stack: "Dropping CSE 101 would put you at 9 units, below full-time. This triggers an SAP review — your Pell Grant of $7,395 requires full-time enrollment, and your Cal Grant GPA verification is due March 2nd. Here's what to do..." On the downgraded stack: "Dropping CSE 101 would put you at 9 units, below full-time. This could affect your financial aid eligibility — including grants that require full-time enrollment. For a detailed breakdown of what's at risk and what to do, start your free trial or check back tomorrow."
Warning messages
Every response on the downgraded stack includes a contextual warning. Not a generic disclaimer — a specific note about what's limited in this response. "This answer covers your academic situation. It may also affect your financial aid. For full analysis, start your free trial or check back tomorrow." The warning connects directly to the trial prompt at the moment of highest friction.
Flagging risks without analyzing them requires almost no reasoning — the model just recognizes that enrollment status connects to financial aid. Any model can do this reliably. The part that requires stronger reasoning is quantifying the risk, calculating the specific impact, and building an action plan. That's what the full stack provides and the downgraded stack withholds. The student gets warned about risks they didn't know about (valuable, builds trust) but the actionable detail is behind the upgrade (drives conversion).
The problem — three layers of deadline complexity
🌐
Federal deadlines — low risk
Stable, well-documented, widely indexed. Examples: FAFSA national deadline, federal loan disbursement windows. Model training data is usually reliable here.
🗺️
State deadlines — medium risk
Change yearly. Examples: Cal Grant GPA verification, state grant programs. Must be fetched fresh every cycle — never rely on training data alone.
🏫
Institutional deadlines — high risk
Every school differs. Examples: UCSD registration windows, specific department deadlines. Not reliably in model training data. Always search before answering.
The solution
Always search first
Every time-sensitive query triggers a web search before the AI call. The result is injected into Layer 3. LumiArc never answers deadline or dollar-amount questions from training data alone.
Cite everything
Every date and dollar figure in a response must include a source citation. Layer 4 enforces this. If live data wasn't fetched, LumiArc tells the student and links to the official source.
Flag uncertainty
When confidence is low, LumiArc says so explicitly: "Based on [source], but verify directly at [url]." Calibrated uncertainty is more trustworthy than false confidence.
Data age check
If fetched data is older than 48 hours, LumiArc flags it to the student. The Layer 3 template always includes the fetch timestamp for this reason.

Free tier AI limits
Rolling 24-hour token budget
Free users get a token budget on a rolling 24-hour window — roughly equivalent to 15 AI conversations. Backend tracks actual token usage. Frontend displays a simplified conversation count with a progress bar. Each message's token cost expires 24 hours after it was sent, so the budget is always refilling. The student is never fully locked out — when the budget is exceeded, the AI downgrades instead of stopping.
Why rolling, not monthly
A monthly reset punishes a student who has one heavy week — they'd wait 3 weeks with degraded AI, long enough to churn. A rolling 24-hour window means heavy usage on Monday doesn't affect Friday. The budget recovers overnight, matching how students naturally use the app — a session today, another tomorrow. Only sustained heavy daily usage hits the wall consistently, and those students are self-identifying as power users most likely to convert.
Budget size~50,000 tokens per 24-hour rolling window. Roughly equivalent to 15 average conversations (mix of Simple through Complex queries).
Frontend displayConversation count + progress bar. No token jargon. "5 conversations remaining" is clear. The bar depletes as they use AI features.
Token weightingA Simple query (~1,000 tokens) uses less budget than a Complex query (~8,300 tokens). The frontend conversation count adjusts dynamically — complex questions cost more "conversations" than simple ones.
All tiers accessibleFree users can access all AI tiers (Simple through Complex) within their budget. They experience the full product, just in limited quantity.

Single threshold — full stack or downgraded stack

The student is either on the full optimized stack (Flash / DeepSeek / GPT-4.1 / GPT-5) or the downgraded stack (DeepSeek only). Decision 4 Deadline database — how to keep it fresh and accurate at scale? ✓ Resolved
Decision: No maintained database — on-demand web search at query time, scheduled background searches for proactive alerts.

Deadline data is public. Every US institution publishes it on their financial aid and registrar pages. studentaid.gov covers federal deadlines. State aid commissions (e.g. CSAC for Cal Grant) cover state deadlines. The problem was never availability — it was the maintenance burden of keeping LumiArc's own copy accurate across thousands of institutions every academic cycle.

The solution is to never maintain a copy at all. For on-demand queries, LumiArc searches official sources at query time and injects the result into Layer 3. For proactive alerts, a Supabase Edge Function runs on a weekly schedule per student, searches for their relevant deadlines, and fires a notification if a threshold is crossed. Official sources are always the ground truth.
Implications
  • On-demand deadline queries always trigger a web search before the AI call — never answered from training data alone
  • Background search runs weekly per student — sufficient cadence since deadlines don't change mid-cycle
  • If a background search fails or returns a bad result, LumiArc defaults to directing the student to the official source rather than firing an uncertain alert
  • No deadline database to build, maintain, or keep current — removes an entire class of operational risk
  • Scales to any US institution without additional data work
Decision 5 International students — how to handle non-US financial aid systems? ✓ Resolved
Decision: Serve international students at US institutions with status-aware feature blocking and targeted scholarship routing. Students at non-US institutions remain explicitly deferred.

International students studying at US institutions are already in LumiArc's launch market. They're at US schools, navigating a financial aid landscape that has no federal aid eligibility for F-1/J-1 visa holders, and they're among the most underserved student segments — no FAFSA, limited or no state aid, dependent entirely on institutional and private scholarships. LumiArc can serve them today with the existing stack. The decision is about what to show, what to block, and what to add.

The residency/citizenship field collected at onboarding already drives this routing. Permanent residents are functionally identical to domestic students — full FAFSA eligibility, no special handling needed. DACA/AB 540 students are already handled via the California Dream Act routing. The new case being resolved here is F-1/J-1 visa holders.
What gets blocked for F-1/J-1 students
  • FAFSA features — hidden entirely, replaced with a clear explanation and link to the school's international student financial aid office
  • Federal grant eligibility checks (Pell, etc.) — blocked
  • Subsidized/unsubsidized federal loan features — blocked
  • Work-study — blocked (F-1 students have strict on-campus work authorization restrictions; LumiArc should not surface this without more nuance)
  • State grant features (Cal Grant, etc.) — blocked for F-1/J-1
What stays the same
  • Document uploads — award letter and scholarship PDFs work identically. Same hybrid parse-and-confirm flow. International institutional aid award letters may not include federal aid fields, but the parser displays what it extracts and the confirm flow handles gaps.
  • Scholarship tracking and deadline alerts — fully available, no changes
  • Academic planning and career prep pillars — no changes at all
What gets added
  • Scholarship references filtered for international students — IEFA, institutional international aid pages, private scholarship databases that don't restrict by citizenship. Same static curated map pattern as domestic, different filter branch based on status.
  • A clear onboarding explanation of what LumiArc can and can't help with for their situation — sets expectations upfront rather than letting students discover blocked features mid-use.
What remains deferred
Students at non-US institutions (Canada, UK, Australia, etc.) involve entirely different aid systems, academic calendars, and degree structures. This is a separate product scope and stays deferred until the US product is proven.
Decision 6 Scholarship reference list — how to build, curate, and keep it current at scale? ✓ Resolved
Decision: Personalized reference links plus student-driven upload flow.

LumiArc doesn't need to maintain a scholarship database. The solution is two-pronged. First, LumiArc provides personalized links to the right doors — school scholarship portal, department-specific page, state aid commission, external databases like Fastweb — filtered against the student's profile. A CS student at UCSD gets the Jacobs School engineering page, not the generic financial aid homepage. Second, once the student finds scholarships, they upload the document into LumiArc. Sonnet parses it, extracts structured records, student confirms, and app logic handles tracking and deadline alerts from there.
Implications
  • No scholarship database to build or maintain — student brings the data in
  • Only 7–11% of students receive scholarships and most have one or two — upload volume is naturally low, no exploitation risk worth engineering against beyond a passive monthly rate limit
  • Reference list is a curated static list filtered by student profile — simpler and more reliable than web search for this purpose
  • LumiArc is honest it's pointing to doors, not opening them
Decision 7 Work-study matching — stay as eligibility + link, or build school job board integration? ✓ Resolved
Decision: Eligibility check, explanation, and link only. No matching feature needed.

Work-study matching was a misframing. Work-study eligibility is determined entirely at FAFSA time — the student either has it in their award or they don't. After that it's simply applying for a campus job through the school's student employment portal, the same as any normal job application. There's no matching complexity to solve. LumiArc's value here is confirming eligibility, explaining what work-study actually is (wages earned through part-time work, not automatic cash — widely misunderstood), and linking directly to the school's student employment portal.
Implications
  • No job board integration needed at any stage — the school's portal handles this
  • LumiArc's job is explanation and routing, not matching
  • Feature is Simple tier, Gemini Flash, fully free
